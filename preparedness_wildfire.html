Preparedness

Wildfire

Wildfires occur in woodland settings in or near forests, rural area, or remote mountain sites. They typically begin unnoticed; usually triggered by lightning but can begin at the hands of an arsonist. They spread very quickly burning brush, trees, and homes.

Meet with your family and create a plan of what to do and where to go. Create your home and its surroundings with safety in mind. Use fire retardant materials. Regularly clean everything (including roof and gutters) and coat with flame retardants. Equip chimneys and stovepipes with spark resistors. Install mesh screens at various places on each level of your home professional installation may be needed. Install dual-sensor smoke alarms throughout your home and be sure to test and replace the batteries regularly. Keep a ladder taller enough to gain roof access within easy reach. Schedule time inspect your home regularly. Consider installing protective shutters or fire resistant drapes as a precaution. Keep exterior items which may burn away from your house – such as lawn furniture, wood piles, BBQ tarps, etc. Ensure you have an alternate water source in the event there is no ‘running’ water available; for example, a pool, pond, well, or drums and remember that this is not drinking water. Keep a garden hose ready at all times that is long enough to reach around your entire house. Consider keeping a transportable gasoline powered pump is case the electrical power is cut off and make sure that all exterior water outlets are protected against freezing. Keep the area immediately adjacent to your home clean of anything that may burn – leaves, grass, limbs, etc. Keep tree limbs 15 ft away from your house . Keep all driveways and entranceways to your home clear so that fire vehicles can get in. Post emergency phone numbers throughout the house and have regular family meetings to rehearse your family plans.

If you see a wildfire and have not received notification already, call 9-1-1. Do not assume someone else has already called it in. If you have time, start preparing your house for the worst. Remove any and all flammable materials as far away from exterior walls as possible. Close all attics, vents, windows, pet doors, shutters, etc. Close all fireplace screens but make sure the vents are open. Make sure that all natural gas, propane, gasoline, and fuel oil sources are securely shut off at the source. Make sure that you leave any and all sprinklers on. Keep a steady stream of water going on the highest spot of your house for as long as you can. Be sure to leave all lights on so that you can see through the smoke. If the fire starts getting close to your house, put your car near an entrance way so that you can quickly escape if you have to. Start the vehicle and leave it running but do not do this if you have the vehicle in an enclosed area. Drive cautiously out and do not speed. You do not want to have an accident while trying to leave. Except for the driver, have all passengers to lie as close to the vehicles floorboard as possible and cover them with a fire retardant material. If you receive an evacuation order – leave do not hesitate. If you are trapped in your home stay away from the outside walls. If you are trapped in an outside area, be sure that you find a sparse fire fuel area (rocky not vegetation). If you are on foot lie face down in a ditch on the uphill side.

If you or a family member are a burn victim, clean the area and cover it. For several hours after the fire, keep a ‘fire watch’ going. Immediately put out any fire type glow that you might see. If you have evacuated, do not go back into the house until a fire department official is on-site and gives the all clear. Just opening a door or a window can create a draft that can cause you to lose everything, including a life. Once is ok to enter, check every inch of the house to look any ‘fire glows’. Be careful walking around your house because you might step into a clump of leaves which has a ‘fire glow’ going underneath which you can’t see and your foot movement opens it up and gives it the air that it needs to ignite. If you detect heat, smoke, of smell of anything burning or even a hint of some type of fuel smell don’t touch it but rather saturate it with water. Follow local orders about handling fire ash and be sure to always wear a mask when cleaning up. Cleaning products, paint, batteries and damaged fuel containers must be disposed of properly to avoid risks. Be sure to wear heavy clothing, gloves and shoes to protect yourself when cleaning up. Make sure that any water or food that you may consume is not contaminated. Take extra caution in the handling of your pets. They might walk through leaves or debris which may have ‘fire glows’ and not know it. Keep a watchful eye out for wild animals who use to live in areas surrounding your home which no longer affords them food and water so they may come to your house for survival. Above all, remain calm and try to have a calming effect on all of your family members.
