Preparedness

Hurricane

A hurricane is a tropical cyclone that occurs in North Atlantic Ocean area. A tropical cyclone is a rapidly-rotating storm system characterized by a low-pressure center, strong winds, and a spiral arrangement of thunderstorms that produce heavy rain. In addition to strong winds and rain, tropical cyclones are capable of generating high waves, damaging storm surge, and tornadoes. They typically weaken rapidly over land where they are cut off from their primary energy source.

Know and understand your surroundings. Know the potential damage to your home from flash floods, tidal flooding, and storm surges. Learn your community evacuation routes and an alternate evacuation route if you are unable to get out quickly enough. Make plans to secure your property. Start with covering all windows and doors with plywood. Install straps and/or clips to secure your roof to the frame structure. Clear loose and clogged rain gutters and downspouts. Reinforce any garage doors. Have a generator ready for emergencies. If in a high rise building do not stay above the 10th floor. Consider building a safe room. Be aware of storm surges as they have the greatest potential for loss of life as related to a hurricane. Storm surges are when ocean water is being pushed ahead of the hurricane by its strong winds. This combines with normal tides to create a storm tide. Many of the major hurricanes such as Camille, Katrina, Hugo and Sandy had storm surges as high as 40 ft at impact with the shoreline. This created complete devastation as this water fell to the ground. Causing many of the buildings which were withstanding the hurricane force winds to crumple because of the erosion cause by the storm surge. Typical homes, roads, buildings, and much of the communities’ infrastructure is simply washed away when this happens. Ensure that you have an adequate supply of food and water.

Secure your home close shutter on the doors and windows. Secure or bring in outdoor furniture. Turn your refrigerator/freezer to its highest setting. Stay tuned to your local radio or TV. Turn off all utilities if instructed to include propane tanks. If you live near the coast where a hurricane is landing, evacuate if directed by the authorities; do not hesitate. If you live in a mobile home find secure temporary housing for you and your family even if you feel that you are securely strapped and fastened to the ground. If you live in a high rise go to the lower floors as the winds are much higher at elevated levels. If you stay, get to the most secure room in your home. Stay indoors and away from windows and doors. Close and secure all interior and exterior doors. Keep all curtains and blinds closed. Do not be fooled if you see clear skies as this may just be the eye of the storm with the strongest winds coming.

Stay tuned to your NOAA radio or the local news. Be aware of potential flooding and extended rainfall after a hurricane. Do not return to your home unless you have specific instructions from the local authorities to do so. Be aware of the assistance you may be able to receive from FEMA: sheltering, housing rental resources, food/water, etc. Know that if you are separated from your family the American Red Cross may assist you as they keep a database of all contacts. Know that if you have texting capabilities you can text “SHELTER” + your zip code to 43362 to locate the nearest shelter. Drive only if absolutely necessary as there may be washed out roads, fallen objects, downed electrical lines, and weakened structures. Move carefully around your home and look for loose power lines, gas leaks, or standing storm water. Do not use candles always use flashlights. Watch your pets and keep them under tight control. Watch out for wild animals; especially snakes.
