Preparedness

Snowstorm

A winter storm is an event in which the varieties of precipitation are formed that only occur at low temperatures, such as snow or sleet, or a rainstorm where ground temperatures are low enough to allow ice to form (i.e. freezing rain). In temperate continental climates, these storms are not necessarily restricted to the winter season, but may occur in the late autumn and early spring as well.

Understand and accept the fact that one of the primary concerns is the winter weather's ability to knock out heat, power and communications services to your home or office, sometimes for days at a time. Heavy snowfall and extreme cold can immobilize an entire region. Make sure that you have an adequate supply of rock salt, sand, snow removal equipment, sufficient heating fuel, and adequate clothing and blankets to keep you warm. Make preparations for your animals. Be sure to winterize your vehicles. Insulate your home as much as possible. Clear rain gutters, repair any roof leaks, insulate pipes, and turn on water on to a trickle. Keep fire extinguishers close by and know how to use them. Know how to shut off water valves in case a pipe breaks. Be careful with any heat producing equipment to make sure that it is properly ventilated; one of the leading causes of injury and death in winter storms is carbon monoxide poisoning along with electric shock and fire. Know your terminology which you might hear on the local news: freezing rain is rain that freezes when it hits the ground. Bad but not the worst. Sleet is rain that turns to ice pellets as it falls and before it hits the ground. Winter storm Watch tells you that you might be in for a winter storm. Caution should be used. Winter storm Warning means that preparation should start because a storm is pending.

Stay indoors and stay warm. If you have to go outside be very careful. If you must shovel snow avoid overworking in the cold. Keep dry at all costs. Watch for signs of frostbite – loss of feeling or skin turning very pale and white. If you see this get medical attention immediately; just warming up doesn’t protect you. You could lose a finger, a toe, or even a nose. Watch for signs of hypothermia; disorientation, memory loss, slurred speech, even exhaustion. Drive only if absolutely necessary. Stay off back roads; be sure someone knows where you’re at, where you’re going, your route, and when you should arrive. Conserve energy keep the thermostat down to a very cool level but not cold. Keep warm clothing on even indoors. If you leave your home turn the thermostat down to a very low temperature but don’t turn it off.

If you have evacuated, be very careful when you reenter your home. Look out for animals who may have taken refuge in your home. Check your utilities immediately upon returning. If you do not have utilities don’t stay. You may get trapped in your home for a very long time and not have utilities. Continue to protect yourself from frostbite and hypothermia by wearing warm, loose fitting, lightweight clothing with several layers and stay inside. Continue to monitor your local or NOAA news for weather alerts. Remember, clear weather may be just a short reprise before bad weather returns. If you have a large snowfall, then be very careful when entering your home as a wide variety of calamities may occur; such as a huge snowfall may occur from the snow piled up on your roof when you shut your door upon entering and trapping you inside or even worse it could trap you at the doorway. Check your home for structural damages. If you must travel always be on alert for road problems.
